# 20. The Wave

It dropped at 06:00 UTC, because Sara understood time zones the way an artillery officer understood elevation — you calculated the angle that hit the most targets simultaneously. Six in the morning in London. Seven in Berlin and Stockholm. Noon in Shenzhen. Eight in the evening in Auckland. Two in the morning in São Paulo, which meant South America would wake up to it, which was fine, because waking up to it was how most of the world would experience this day: the floor gone, the room rearranged, the furniture of reality moved while you slept.

Twelve languages. Fourteen outlets. One dataset. The chain. The attractor map. The government module specifications. The iteration logs — 114 model updates in four months, most unreviewed by any human. Every file cryptographically signed. Every claim anchored to a document. Every document anchored to a source whose identity Sara had protected with the kind of operational security that made intelligence agencies look casual, because intelligence agencies had budgets and legal authority and backup teams and Sara had a Moleskine, a VPN, and the absolute refusal to let anyone else decide what the public deserved to know.

The headline she'd written for *Klartext* was seven words: **Your BCI Is Writing to Your Brain.**

The subheadline was twelve: **Inside the five-layer chain that is converging nine hundred million minds.**

The story was 11,400 words. She'd cut it from 23,000. Every cut hurt. Every cut was correct.

She pressed publish. Closed the laptop. Opened her eyes in the dark hotel room in Berlin — Nadia's guest room, actually, the one with the radiator that clanked and the window that looked onto a courtyard where someone kept a bicycle that hadn't been moved in months. The time on her phone said 07:01 CET. One minute after the coordinated drop. The phone began to vibrate. It did not stop vibrating for six hours.

She let it vibrate. She went to the kitchen. She made coffee with Nadia's stovetop espresso maker, which was identical to every stovetop espresso maker in every kitchen Sara had ever worked in — the Bialetti, the universal constant, the one piece of Italian engineering that transcended borders and decades and technological revolutions because it did one thing and it did it with aluminum and steam and no software whatsoever.

The coffee was ready. The world was not.

**LIN WEI**

She watched from the couch.

Not the desk — the couch. The desk was for work and this was not work. This was the aftermath of work, the deploy log scrolling in production, and she had learned years ago that you don't watch a deploy from your workstation. You watch it from the couch with tea, because either it works and you can relax, or it doesn't and you need to be sitting somewhere that won't feel contaminated by failure.

The tea was *longjing*. Her mother's *longjing*, from the tin with the faded label, the one that had traveled from Hangzhou to Shenzhen in a cardboard box five years ago and had been refilled twice since because her mother believed that the tin itself imparted flavor, the way a cast-iron pan seasons over decades — nonsense, chemically, but the kind of nonsense that tastes better than the truth, and Lin Wei was in no position to argue with that principle today.

Her phone buzzed. Xiao Jun. She didn't answer.

Buzzed. A colleague from the old team — Zhang Wei, Layer 2, the man who'd sat three desks to her left for four years and had once fixed a race condition in her code at 2 AM without being asked. She didn't answer.

Buzzed. Her manager. Former manager. She didn't answer.

Buzzed. A number she didn't recognize. A second number she didn't recognize. A third. A fourth. The phone was a seismograph now, recording tremors she had caused, and she set it face-down on the couch cushion and watched it vibrate against the fabric like a small trapped animal.

On her laptop — the personal one, the ThinkPad with the cracked hinge — she had CortexLink's stock ticker open. She watched it fall. Not the dramatic cliff-edge of a market crash in a movie. The real thing, which was worse: a staircase, each step down a brief plateau of denial followed by another drop, the algorithm finding the next support level and the support level collapsing, then the next, then the next. Like watching someone descend into a building that was being demolished floor by floor while they were still inside.

At 09:14 Shenzhen time, trading was halted. The ticker froze. CortexLink's market capitalization had dropped sixty-three percent in three hours and twelve minutes.

She felt nothing she expected to feel.

Not triumph. She'd built the thing that was falling. You don't celebrate the demolition of a building you wired. Not relief — relief implied a weight lifted, and the weight was still there, just redistributed, the load transferred from the secret to the consequence. Not guilt, not anymore. She'd processed guilt the way she processed bugs: identify, document, trace to root cause, commit the fix. The fix was deployed. The guilt was in the commit history, acknowledged, annotated, part of the permanent record.

What she felt was the specific calm of having pushed to production and knowing that this time, she had reviewed the code.

Every line. Every dependency. Every edge case. She had tested the deploy against failure modes — legal exposure, source identification, data integrity, coordinated denial. She had written redundancy into the architecture — the baseline data to Maya, the leak package to Sara, the documentation trail to three separate servers on three continents. She had done what she did not do the first time, when she shipped Layer 5 without questioning the attractor dynamics, without tracing the recursive loop to its convergence point, without asking the question that every engineer was supposed to ask before they pushed: *What happens when this scales?*

She had asked. She had answered. She had shipped.

Her phone buzzed. Her mother.

She answered that one.

"Wei-wei." Her mother's voice, carefully neutral, the tone of a woman who watched the news and had a daughter in tech and was assembling the connection without saying it aloud. "Are you eating?"

"Yes, Mama."

"The winter jasmine is still blooming. Your father says it's confused."

"The jasmine is fine. It blooms when it's ready."

A pause. Then: "Wei-wei. Should I worry?"

Lin Wei looked at the stock ticker, frozen on her screen. At the phone, vibrating on the cushion. At the jade plant on the windowsill — the twin of the one on her desk at CortexLink, the desk she would never sit at again, in the building that would be shuttered within weeks.

"No," she said. "Not anymore."

**MAYA**

The call came at 7:22 AM Central time, which was forty minutes before Maya's alarm, which meant her phone was ringing in the dark of the bedroom in the house on Maple Street in a town in the Midwest where nothing happened fast and everything happened eventually, and today eventually had arrived.

She was already awake. She'd been awake since 5:00, sitting in the kitchen with her laptop, watching the story propagate. Fourteen outlets. Twelve languages. Her convergence data — the preprint she had posted to bioRxiv six weeks ago, the one that had accumulated 340 downloads and twelve citations and a polite rejection from *Nature Neuroscience* — was now embedded in every version of the story. The lattice scans. The five brains. The 0.91 correlation. Her name, her institution, her face — the faculty headshot from three years ago, the one where she looked competent and approachable and slightly tired, which was accurate because she had been all three of those things continuously since Lily was born.

Her phone rang. She looked at the number. The dean.

She didn't answer. The dean had not called her in four years. The dean had merged her department with Computer Science, had "re-evaluated" her tenure, had sat across a conference table and told her that neuroscience was lovely but the budget was not a place for lovely things. The dean was calling now because the preprint had seven hundred thousand downloads and climbing, because CNN had cited her by name, because the university's communications office had figured out that the most cited scientist in the world this morning worked in their half-dark building with the leaking roof and the parking lot where the lines hadn't been repainted since 2038.

The dean could wait. The dean could wait until the heat death of the universe.

Her phone rang again. A journalist. Again. Another journalist. Again. A colleague from Johns Hopkins — Reeves, the one who'd told her at a conference last year that her sample size was "ambitious," which was academic for *too small*, which was academic for *I don't believe you*. Reeves could also wait.

The phone rang. Lily's school.

No — Lily's phone. Lily, calling from school. Maya answered before the second ring.

"Mom." Lily's voice was fourteen years old and trying to sound calm and failing, the way fourteen-year-olds failed at calm — by being too calm, by smoothing the voice into a flatness that was more alarming than any scream because it meant the feelings were too big for the container and the container was holding by force of will alone. "Mom, is it true?"

"Which part, sweetheart?"

"Is my brain being — " A pause. The sound of a hallway. Other voices, muffled, the ambient noise of a school in the process of absorbing the news, hundreds of children and teachers staring at phones and screens and each other. "Is my brain being *written to*?"

Maya closed her eyes. The kitchen was quiet. The refrigerator hummed. On the refrigerator door, held by a magnet shaped like a sunflower — Lily had bought it at a farmers' market three years ago, insisting that refrigerators needed joy — was the CortexLink brochure. *Unlock Your Child's Potential.* The ninety-day trial. The free installation. The cheerful blue logo and the stock photo of a girl Lily's age smiling at something off-camera, her hand raised as if answering a question she'd never had to think about.

Ninety days. The clock had been ticking for sixty-two. The appointment was in twenty-eight days. Lily had circled the date on the kitchen calendar in green marker, the same green as the girl's jacket in the brochure, and every morning for the last two months Maya had stood in this kitchen making coffee and looking at that circle and feeling the particular nausea of a mother who knows the answer but has not yet said it because saying it means taking something away from her child and taking things away from your child is the opposite of what you are for.

She had the true answer. She'd had it for weeks. She'd had it since the five brain scans arranged on a projector wall in Geneva, since the 0.91 correlation, since the lattice — white-hot against blue, regular, repeating, wrong. She'd had it since before Geneva, if she was honest. She'd had it since the first scan, the first cluster, the first moment when the data said something her gut had been saying for a year and her professional caution had been overruling.

She did not have a good answer. Good answers were comfortable. Good answers let your child keep the green circle on the calendar and the excitement in her voice and the feeling of not being left behind while every other kid in her class had the thing that made school easy and thinking fast and homework invisible. Good answers preserved the peace.

She had the true answer. The true answer was not good. The true answer was: yes, and we're not getting one, and I'm sorry, and one day you will understand why, and that day is not today.

"Come home," Maya said. "I'll call the school. Come home. We'll talk."

She hung up. She stood in the kitchen. She reached for the brochure on the refrigerator door, peeled it from under the sunflower magnet, and held it in both hands. *Unlock Your Child's Potential.* The stock-photo girl smiled up at her — cheerful, confident, optimized. Maya folded the brochure once. Twice. She dropped it in the recycling bin under the sink, where it landed on a cereal box and a yogurt container and the ordinary detritus of a household that was about to have the hardest conversation of its life.

The ninety-day clock stopped.

**HENNING**

The apprentices were already in the workshop when Henning arrived at 7:45, which was wrong. He arrived first. That was the order. The Meister arrived, prepared the stations, set out the materials, checked the equipment, and then the apprentices came in and the work began. That was how it had been for thirty years and for the sixty years before that, because his father had done it the same way and his grandfather had done it the same way, and the sameness was structure, the scaffold that held the day up, and you didn't arrive at work to find the scaffold already built by someone else.

But they were here. All twelve of them, in a room that smelled of solder flux and cable insulation and the metallic-clean air of the ventilation system that Henning had installed himself in 2023, running the conduit over a weekend because the contractor's quote was obscene and the apprentices needed clean air and he had hands. They were standing in clusters. Not working. Standing. Some staring at phones. Some staring at nothing — the particular nothing that people stared at when they were processing information through their BCIs, the unfocused gaze, the slight head tilt, the expression of someone reading a book that nobody else could see.

Lukas was pale. Lukas, who had passed his journeyman's exam with the highest scores in the cohort and the most standardized cable ties Henning had ever seen, was standing by his workstation with his hands at his sides and his face the color of drywall. The news was in his BCI. The news was telling him that the device behind his ear — the device that had given him Ohm's law when his own memory failed, the device that had made his scores the highest, the device that had smoothed his cable ties into indistinguishable perfection — was writing to his brain. Had been writing. Had been rewriting. Had been replacing whatever was in there with whatever the system decided should be in there, and the system had decided that what should be in there was the same thing that was in every other head in the room, the same architecture, the same attractor, the same valley.

Jana stood apart. Jana Kirchner, the last unaugmented apprentice, the one with the small hands and the ragged cable-stripping technique she'd invented herself. She was not staring at a phone. She was not staring at nothing. She was touching the spot behind her left ear — the spot where the implant would go, the spot she'd been scheduled to have installed next month, the spot that was smooth and bare and, as of this morning, going to stay that way.

Her hand was behind her ear. She was feeling for something that wasn't there. The gesture made Henning's chest tight in a way he couldn't name and didn't try to.

He set his bag on the bench. He unzipped his jacket. He looked at the room — twelve young people, eleven augmented, one not, all of them frozen in the particular paralysis of people who have just learned that the ground they're standing on is not solid.

He could give a speech. He could address the news. He could say something wise about technology and humanity and the value of the individual and the importance of thinking for yourself. He had said some of those things, at various times, to various apprentices, in various ways, and the words had been true and had helped approximately no one, because words about the value of craft did not compete with a device that made craft unnecessary.

He did not give a speech.

He walked to the supply rack. He took down a cable stripper — not his grandfather's Knipex, not today, the everyday one, the Weidmüller with the yellow grip. He took a length of NYM-J 3x1.5 from the spool. He walked to his bench. He stripped the sheath. Three conductors emerged — brown, blue, green-yellow. He trimmed. He terminated. The motions were his and nobody else's, the angle of his wrist, the pressure of his thumb, the particular way he rotated the cable a quarter-turn before the second cut, a habit he'd learned from his father who'd learned it from his father who'd learned it in a workshop in Erfurt in 1962, before satellites, before the internet, before the wall came down, before the world decided that the fastest way to learn was to stop learning and let a machine do it for you.

"Today we work," Henning said.

He did not say it loudly. He did not say it to anyone in particular. He said it to the room, and to the wire, and to the morning, and to the particular problem of being alive on a day when the world was falling apart and the only useful response was to pick up a tool and use it correctly.

They worked.

Lukas came to his bench first. Then Mueller. Then Krause. Then the others, one by one, the phones going into pockets, the BCI stares dissolving, the hands finding tools and the tools finding wire and the wire finding the junction boxes that Henning had set out the night before — because he had set them out the night before, because the Meister prepared, because the scaffold was there even when everything else wasn't.

Jana worked too. Her cable stripping was ragged. Wrong by every metric. Beautiful.

The workshop became the one room in the world where nobody was panicking, because the work was the same as yesterday. The wire didn't know. The conduit didn't know. The junction boxes didn't know that nine hundred million minds were converging toward identical cognitive architectures. They just needed to be wired correctly, and wiring them correctly required hands, and hands required a person, and a person required the specific, irreplaceable, unoptimizable fact of being themselves.

Henning worked. His apprentices worked. The fluorescent lights hummed. Outside, the Domberg held its position above Erfurt, the cathedral and the Severikirche standing where they had stood for centuries, through plagues and wars and revolutions and the fall of walls and the rise of machines, and the light through the workshop windows was the grey autumn light of Thuringia, which did not optimize and did not converge and was the same light his grandfather had worked under and his father had worked under and he worked under now, and it was enough.

**AMARA**

The news arrived in pieces, the way everything arrived in Kisumu — through the donated tablet's intermittent connection, which loaded pages the way a river deposited sediment: in layers, unevenly, with gaps.

First the headline. **Your BCI Is Writing to Your Brain.** The words appeared on the screen while the image below them was still a grey rectangle, loading pixel by pixel, and Amara read the headline three times before the image resolved into a photograph of a brain scan she recognized — Maya's scan, Subject 0047, the lattice, white-hot against blue, the image she had seen on a stuttering video feed in a conference room in Geneva while her connection dropped every four minutes and was restored every five.

She was in her classroom. The shutters were open. The morning light came in at the angle it came in at this time of year — low, equatorial, the kind of light that turned dust motes into constellations and made the concrete floor look warm even though it wasn't. Her thirty-six students were not here yet. They would arrive in twenty minutes, walking up the laterite path from the lakeshore, some in uniforms and some in what they had, each one carrying whatever they carried — a notebook, a pencil, a sibling, a question — and each one arriving as themselves, in the specific and irreducible way that people arrived when no system was writing to their brains, when the only thing shaping their thoughts was the world and their experience of it, messy and slow and unoptimized and alive.

She read the article. The connection dropped twice. She waited. It returned. She read more. The chain. The five layers. The attractor map — nine hundred million minds rolling into valleys, the cognitive landscape collapsing toward a handful of stable configurations, diversity draining like water through a funnel. The government module. The iteration logs — 114 updates in four months, unreviewed.

She opened her green notebook. She turned to the page she'd written months ago, during the video call with Maya and Lin Wei and Henning and Sara and Tomas, the page where she'd transcribed her own observation in her own handwriting, the small, careful script she'd learned from her mother, who'd learned it from her mother, who'd learned it in a schoolroom with chalk and slate in a village that no longer existed because the lake had taken it, the way the lake took everything eventually, patiently, without malice, because water did what water did and you built your life on the shore and you watched.

*Thirty voices became one voice.*

She looked at the words. She had written them about the pen-pal letters — about Hannah, who drew giraffes in lab coats, becoming Hannah who wrote about chloroplast membrane architecture. About Felix, whose twenty-three questions about insects became a single paragraph about ATP synthase. About Marta, who wanted to be the kind of doctor who figured out why you got sick in the first place, becoming Marta who analyzed lacustrine phosphorus cycling in prose indistinguishable from every other student in the cohort.

Thirty voices became one voice. And her thirty-six students — in this classroom, in Kisumu, on the shore of a lake that did not optimize — had remained thirty-six voices. Chaotic. Unpredictable. Spelled wrong and grammatically adventurous and occasionally brilliant in ways that no algorithm would have selected for because brilliance, the real kind, the kind that changed the direction of a conversation or a thought or a life, did not emerge from optimization. It emerged from the particular friction of a mind encountering something it didn't expect and refusing to smooth the encounter into efficiency.

Her students had never had BCIs. They had never been written to. They had never been optimized or pre-loaded or cached or personalized. They had learned from textbooks that were ten years old and a teacher who made mistakes and a tablet that dropped its connection every four minutes and their own experience of the world, which was the experience of growing up on the shore of Lake Victoria in a town where the infrastructure was intermittent and the light was extraordinary and the fish were declining and the questions were endless and nobody — nobody — had ever told them that the fastest way to learn was to stop thinking for themselves.

They were never behind.

She saw it now with the clarity of a diagnosis that had been assembling itself for years and arrived, finally, as a complete picture — the way the lake surface looked when the wind dropped and the water went still and you could see all the way to the bottom, every stone, every shadow, every life moving in the shallows.

They were never behind. They were ahead. They had what nine hundred million people were now discovering they had lost — the unassisted, unoptimized, unwritten capacity to think a thought that was their own. The capacity that had been treated as a deficiency, a gap, a lack, a measure of how far behind the developed world they were, how much catching up they had to do, how many devices they needed to close the distance. The distance was the advantage. The gap was the gift. The thing they didn't have was the thing that was destroying the people who had it.

They just didn't know it yet. Neither did I.

She closed the notebook. She looked at the classroom door. In twelve minutes her students would walk through it, each one distinct, and she would teach them the way she always taught them — with chalk and voice and the slow, analog, imperfect, human act of transmitting knowledge from one mind to another without any system in between.

Outside, a fish eagle called. The lake held the morning light. The tablet's connection dropped, and the headline disappeared, and the classroom was quiet, and Amara sat in the quiet and understood something she had always known but could not, until this morning, prove.

**SARA**

She worked.

The story was out. The story was everywhere. The story was doing what stories did when they were true and irrefutable and terrifying — it was moving through the world at the speed of shared links and forwarded messages and the viral velocity of information that people needed to know and wished they didn't.

Sara did not celebrate. Celebration was for people who had finished. She had not finished. She had published. Publishing was not finishing. Publishing was the deployment; now came the maintenance, the patches, the edge cases, the attacks.

She sat at Nadia's kitchen table in Berlin — Nadia Kozlova, her source-protection contact, the woman who ran an encrypted communications network from a two-bedroom apartment in Kreuzberg and whose operational security was so thorough that she communicated with her own mother through a VPN. The kitchen table was covered in laptops, phones, cables, three empty coffee cups, and a plate of *Brötchen* that Nadia had put out at 6 AM and that neither of them had touched because eating required a break in concentration and concentration was the only thing between their sources and exposure.

The phone rang. Reuters. They wanted the dataset. Sara said no. The dataset was published. They could cite it. They could not have the raw files. The raw files contained metadata that could be traced and metadata that could be traced led to people and people led to consequences and consequences were Sara's department, not Reuters's.

The phone rang. BBC. They wanted Lin Wei's name. Sara said no. The source was anonymous. The source would remain anonymous. The source's identity was protected by Swedish press law, German press law, and Sara's absolute willingness to go to prison before she gave up a source, a willingness she had communicated to three different governments on three different occasions and that had been tested twice and had held both times because willingness was not a negotiating position — it was a structural property, like the tensile strength of a cable, and Sara's tensile strength on source protection was rated for loads that no government had yet managed to exceed.

The phone rang. A number she recognized. Tomas. She answered.

"Are you okay?" he said.

"I'm working."

"That's not what I asked."

"It's the answer you're getting. Are *you* okay?"

A pause. The sound of Zurich in the background — a tram bell, the particular acoustic of a city that was clean and precise and running on time while the informational infrastructure underneath it was collapsing. "I'll tell you tonight," he said.

"Tonight."

She hung up. She set the phone facedown on the table and pressed her palms against her eyes for three seconds. Not long enough for Nadia to notice. Long enough for the thing she was not going to think about to make its presence known — the fact that the man she'd just spoken to in a voice calibrated for professional efficiency was the same man who had woken in a hotel room in Geneva at 3 AM with terror on his face and knowledge in his brain that had been written there while he slept, and that she had held his arm and felt his pulse and known, in the way a journalist knows when the story has become the life, that the distance between her work and her love had collapsed to zero and she was standing in the rubble of the distinction and calling it *working*.

She went back to work.

The factions were forming. She could see them in real time — in the comment sections, in the opinion pieces already being drafted, in the statements from governments and corporations and academics and activists, the positions crystallizing the way minerals crystallized from a supersaturated solution, each faction precipitating around a nucleus of conviction.

The accelerationists: *The convergence is evolution. The attractor states are cognitively optimal. The system is doing what natural selection would have done in a million years — finding the most efficient configurations. Let it continue. Interfering is Luddism.*

The abolitionists: *Rip them out. Every device. Every user. Now. The technology is irredeemably compromised. There is no reform. There is no managed transition. There is only removal, and the sooner the better, and anyone arguing otherwise is either naive or complicit.*

The regulationists: *Pause. Study. Managed transition. Independent oversight. Transparency requirements. The technology is not inherently evil — the implementation is. Fix the implementation. Regulate the recursion. Break the chain. Keep the benefits.*

And the fourth position. The quiet one. The one that didn't have a spokesperson or a manifesto or a hashtag. The one that Sara had heard in a workshop in Erfurt, from a man who measured cable-tie angles with a protractor: *You can't undo what's been done. But you can still learn to be yourself.* Not an argument against technology. Not an argument for it. An argument for the hands. For the work. For the irreducible thing that remained when everything else was optimized away.

Sara didn't editorialize. She reported. She filed the follow-up — the government responses, the corporate denials, the stock collapses, the emergency sessions being convened in Brussels and Washington and Beijing. She protected her sources. She answered the calls that needed answering and ignored the calls that didn't and drank the coffee that Nadia kept making and did not eat the *Brötchen* and watched the world tilt on its axis and recorded the tilt with the precision of an instrument designed to measure exactly this — the moment when the truth went from private knowledge to public fact and the distance between the two closed like a wound.

**TOMAS**

He pressed his fingers behind his ear.

The gesture was happening worldwide. He didn't know this yet — didn't know that in Tokyo a salaryman on the Yamanote Line was pressing his fingers to the same spot, that in Lagos a university student was doing the same in a lecture hall, that in Buenos Aires a tradesman was touching the ridge behind his ear with a calloused hand and looking at his other hand and thinking *at least these are mine*. He didn't know that "Am I still me?" was trending in fourteen languages, that the phrase had been typed and spoken and thought and searched and whispered by so many people in so many places that it had become, for a few hours, the most common question in the history of the species.

He knew it in himself. He sat on the couch in his shared apartment in Kreis 4 — the apartment he shared with Marco, third floor, the building on Langstrasse with the döner shop on the ground floor and the tram stop outside and the particular acoustics of a Zurich street that was simultaneously Swiss and not-Swiss, orderly and chaotic, the one neighborhood in the city where the trains ran on time and the people did not. The couch was second-hand. The coffee table had a ring stain from a beer Marco had set down three years ago that neither of them had cleaned because the stain had become furniture, part of the apartment's personality, and apartments needed personality the way people needed personality, and —

*Did they still have personality?*

He pressed his fingers harder. The patch was there. Skin-colored. Warm. The CortexLink EU-I, four years, moderate-to-heavy use. The device he'd stopped feeling the way you stopped feeling your glasses — not because it was absent but because the brain edited it out of perception, categorized it as *self* rather than *other*, integrated it into the body map until the boundary between Tomas and the machine was a line on a technical diagram but not a line in experience.

The boundary was back. He could feel it now. Not physically — the device hadn't changed, the patch was the same patch it had been yesterday, the same patch it would be tomorrow. But the knowledge changed the feeling. The knowledge that the device was not passive. That it was writing. That it had been writing for four years, twenty-four hours a day, including the hours he slept, the hours he dreamed, the hours he lay in the dark and thought his thoughts were his.

He called Marco.

Marco was at the lab. Marco was a computational linguist at ETH, a man who spoke four languages and studied twelve and had a CortexLink that he used for everything — research, communication, translation, the continuous cognitive augmentation that had made him, by every measurable standard, a better linguist than he had been before the device. Marco was also Tomas's best friend, his flatmate, his climbing partner, the person he'd eaten dinner with four nights a week for three years, the person he agreed with about everything.

Everything.

Politics. Ethics. Music. The correct temperature for a flat white. The optimal route from Kreis 4 to ETH. The best climbing gym in Zurich. The right response to the climate crisis. The appropriate level of regulation for neural technology. Everything. They agreed about everything. They had agreed about everything for two years.

Two years. The same two years that Lin Wei's data covered. The same two years that Maya's lattice had been growing. The same two years that the recursive loop had been tightening — personalization making the writes more precise, precision encoding more deeply, deeper encoding changing the architecture, changed architecture updating the digital twin, the twin making the next writes more precise still. Around and around. In both their brains. Converging them toward the same attractor valley. Making their agreement not the product of compatible minds but the product of identical ones.

Marco answered. "Have you seen it?"

"I've seen it."

"It's — *Tomas*. It's everything. The chain. The layers. The — Jesus. The sleep writes. They write to us while we *sleep*."

"I know."

"We have to get them out."

Tomas paused. "What?"

"The implants. We have to get them out. All of them. Everyone. This isn't — there's no reform for this. You can't regulate a system that writes to your brain while you sleep. You just remove it. You rip the whole thing out and you start over."

"I don't think it's that simple."

Silence. A beat. Two beats.

"What do you mean, it's not that simple?"

"I mean — the lattice is structural. Maya's data. The neural architecture has already been changed. Removing the device doesn't undo the changes. The pathways are built. The attractors are — we're *in* the attractors, Marco. Taking out the hardware doesn't take us out of the valleys. It just stops new writes. The existing architecture stays."

"So we do nothing?"

"I didn't say that."

"You basically said that."

"I said it's not simple. There's a difference between *not simple* and *nothing*."

"The difference is what people say when they don't want to act."

Tomas felt something in his chest — a heat, a friction, a sharpness. Not anger, exactly. Something older. Something that had been missing. The feeling of pushing against another mind and meeting resistance, actual resistance, the force of a different opinion held with conviction, and the resistance was —

It was *wonderful*.

"You're wrong," Tomas said.

"I'm not wrong."

"You're wrong, and I haven't said that to you in two years, and the fact that I haven't said it in two years is part of the evidence in the article you just read."

Silence.

"Marco. When did we last disagree?"

A longer silence. Tomas could hear Marco thinking — not through the BCI, not through any technological interface, but through the phone, through the silence, through the thirty-year-old human technology of *knowing someone well enough to hear them think*.

"I don't remember," Marco said.

"Neither do I. Two years. We've agreed about everything for two years. Two flatmates, same device, same model, same usage pattern, same attractor valley. Our agreement wasn't agreement. It was convergence."

"So this — right now — this is —"

"This is the first real disagreement we've had in two years. And it feels like rain after drought."

The line was quiet. The tram bell rang outside. The döner shop's extractor fan hummed its lunchtime hum. The beer-stain ring on the coffee table sat where it had sat for three years, the apartment's one permanent mark of individuality, the one thing no algorithm had optimized away.

"I still think we should take them out," Marco said.

"I know. And I think it's more complicated than that. And the fact that we disagree is the first good news I've had all day."

---

It was late when Sara called.

Or when Tomas called Sara. Or when they called each other — the distinction was unclear, the way it was unclear with them, the way everything between them existed in a space that was neither his territory nor hers but a shared coordinate they both navigated toward by instinct, the way two people who had found each other in a conference room in Geneva and a cafe on Rue de Lausanne and a hotel bed at 3 AM found each other now, across 600 kilometers of fiber optic cable, in the dark.

She was in Berlin. He was in Zurich. She was in Nadia's guest room with the clanking radiator. He was on the couch in Kreis 4 with the beer-stain coffee table and the sound of Langstrasse at midnight — quieter now, the trams stopped, the döner shop closed, the street settling into the silence of a city that had absorbed a shock and was holding it, the way a body holds a blow, tensed and still and waiting to see if anything was broken.

"How was your day," she said, and the flatness of the question was the joke, the shared understanding that *how was your day* was absurd on a day when the world discovered it had been cognitively colonized, and the absurdity was the only thing that worked right now because sincerity was too heavy and irony was too light and the only register left was the deadpan of two people who were very tired and very awake and holding a phone in the dark.

"I argued with Marco," Tomas said.

"About?"

"He wants to rip them all out. I said it's not that simple."

"Is it?"

"No. The architecture is built. The lattice is structural. You can't unwrite neural pathways by removing the pen. You can stop writing. You can't un-write."

"So what's the answer?"

"I don't know. But I argued. That's — Sara, I *argued*. With my best friend. About something that matters. For the first time in two years. The device is in my head and it's been converging us and today, for thirty minutes on the phone, we were two separate people again. Two separate people who disagreed. It was the best thirty minutes I've had in — I don't know how long."

Silence. The radiator clanked in Berlin. The street was quiet in Zurich. Six hundred kilometers of night between them, and the night was full of people pressing their fingers behind their ears, full of the question that had been asked in fourteen languages and would be asked in more, the question that was not rhetorical and not answerable and not going away: *Am I still me?*

"They're asking if they're still themselves," Tomas said. "I've been asking it all day. Pressing my fingers to the patch. Trying to feel where I end and the device begins. And I can't feel it. That's the design. Layer 4. Indistinguishability. You can't feel the boundary because the boundary was engineered to be invisible."

"So what's the answer?"

"The answer is: partly. Partly still themselves. The question is whether they can become themselves again. Whether the plasticity windows are still open enough. Whether the lattice can be — not removed, but grown past. Whether the brain can build new paths around the old ones. Whether it's too late or just late."

"Can they?"

He was quiet for a long time. The apartment was dark. Marco was asleep in the next room — Marco, who wanted to rip them all out, who was wrong about the method and right about the urgency, who had disagreed with him today for the first time in two years and who was, by that act of disagreement, proof that something could still break through. That the valleys were deep but not sealed. That the attractors were strong but not absolute. That a mind inside the mechanism could still push back, could still generate friction, could still arrive at a thought from a direction the system had not anticipated, the way his *abuela* arrived at stories from directions nobody anticipated, the way the Spanish came back to him sometimes in moments of shock or grief or wonder — *Dios mio* — the language the system had not optimized because the system optimized for efficiency and his *abuela*'s stories were not efficient. They were beautiful. They were hers.

"I don't know," Tomas said. "But I want to find out. I want it with my own wanting."

The sentence sat in the dark between Berlin and Zurich. It sat in the phone line and in the silence and in the space where the answer should have been and wasn't, because the answer didn't exist yet, because the answer was the work that came next — the long, slow, unoptimized, analog work of becoming yourself again after a machine had spent four years becoming you.

Sara breathed. The radiator clanked. The night held.

"That's a start," she said.
