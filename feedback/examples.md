# Examples: De-Robotizing the Prose

This document provides "Before and After" comparisons to illustrate the transition from "Maximum Probability" LLM prose to "Human-Centric" narrative. 

---

## 1. Eliminating the "Philosophical Coda"
**The Problem:** LLMs love to end scenes by explaining the "thematic meaning."
**The Fix:** End on a sharp, sensory image. Let the reader feel the weight without being told what it is.

* **LLM Version:** "The rain washed the blood from the pavement, turning the neon reflections into a blur of red and blue. He stood there for a moment, thinking of what he had lost. It was a silent reminder that in this city, even the brightest lights cast the darkest shadows."
* **Human-Centric Rewrite:** "The rain hit the pavement, thinning the blood until it was just a pink smear in a neon puddle. He didn't move. A passing hover-cab kicked up a spray of oily water, soaking his boots. He just watched the red light of the 'Open' sign flicker, then die."

---

## 2. Removing "Double-Beat" Adjectives & Adverbs
**The Problem:** Using paired descriptors (e.g., *cold and unforgiving*) creates a predictable, "glassy" rhythm.
**The Fix:** Use one specific, unusual noun or verb instead of two generic adjectives.

* **LLM Version:** "The terminal was **old and battered**, its screen flickering with a **weak and ghostly** light. He typed **quickly and nervously**, hoping the security wouldn't catch him."
* **Human-Centric Rewrite:** "The terminal's casing was held together by yellowed duct tape. On the screen, the cursor blinked—a sickly green pulse that made his eyes ache. His fingers tripped over the keys, leaving damp smudges on the plastic."

---

## 3. Subtext vs. "The Because Trap"
**The Problem:** Explaining why a character does something (the "because" clause) kills the mystery.
**The Fix:** Show the physical reaction. Delete the explanation.

* **LLM Version:** "Kael looked away from the monitor **because he couldn't bear to see the data**, knowing that his life was about to change forever."
* **Human-Centric Rewrite:** "Kael stared at the monitor until his vision blurred. He looked at the ceiling, tracing a water stain that looked like a map of a city he'd never visit. He didn't look back at the screen."

---

## 4. Injecting "The Dirt" (Sensory Grit)
**The Problem:** Descriptions are often "clean" or "safe" (e.g., *the air was stale*).
**The Fix:** Use specific, jagged sensory details.

* **LLM Version:** "The Checkpoint was crowded. People waited in long lines, their faces filled with worry as the guards watched them closely."
* **Human-Centric Rewrite:** "The Checkpoint smelled of ozone and unwashed wool. A hum vibrated through the floorboards—constant, teeth-rattling. In the queue, a man in a frayed coat wouldn't stop scratching a patch of eczema on his neck. The guards didn't look at faces; they looked at IDs and trigger guards."

---

## 5. De-Gluing Dialogue
**The Problem:** Characters "nodding," "sighing," or "smiling knowingly" to keep the flow smooth.
**The Fix:** Use silence, interruption, or dismissive actions.

* **LLM Version:** "’We have to go,’ she said, nodding urgently. ‘If we stay here, they will find us.’ 
    He sighed and looked at her. ‘I know,’ he replied softly."
* **Human-Centric Rewrite:** "’Move,’ she said. She was already checking the charge on her pulse-kit. ‘Unless you want to meet the sweep-team.’
    He didn't move. He just watched a cockroach navigate the edge of a discarded food carton. ‘I'm tired of running.’"
